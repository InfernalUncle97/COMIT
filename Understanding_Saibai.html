Background

Saibai is home to around 340 Koeybuway and Moegibuway people, with 85.6% identifying as Torres Strait Islanders (ABS, 2021). In recent decades, the island has long relied on imported diesel from the mainland paying upwards of $3.70/litre diesel compared to mainland price of $1.90/litre (EWB, 2024a), coupled with frequent flooding their energy infrastructure and supply is fragile and unreliable. These disruptions create profound consequences for residents as they spoil food stored in communal freezers, interrupt digital classrooms for young students and threaten the health of Elders who are depended on powered medical care.

The islands geography and climate are the driving factor of why these problems are common for Saibai, where the island is incredibly flat sitting about a meter above sea level. Each year, they've experienced storm surges and king tide wash across the land in turn damaging buildings fuel storage and electrical systems (TSIRC, 2025). With very little dry ground majority swamp, the community also struggles with overcrowding with their growing population and diminishing housing. Where the latest Master Plan estimate that the island is short about 38 houses for the coming decade (TSIRC, 2025). Because of this land shortage, traditional ground based solar farms or any electrical farm for that matter wont fit or prove as a viable option for Saibai, as they'd take up space better used for homes or cultural areas not to mention costly flood protection for land based solutions.

Community-led Approach & Floating Solar Fit

Throughout our research, we've closely examined where there has been some progress outside of the Torres Strait such as the Masig Island's hybrid solar-diesel microgrid, proving renewables can work in remote areas similar to Saibai Islands (Mirabou Energy, 2023). But even there, maintence is dependent on outside contractors having to be flown in and out.

That’s where floating solar technology comes in. These systems sit on buoyant modular platforms anchored just offshore. The surrounding seawater naturally cools the panels, helping them run more efficiently, and the setup can link into the existing diesel microgrid to keep power consistent.

Most importantly, it leaves precious land free for housing and cultural purposes while cutting down fuel imports and emissions. Problem Description

The current underlining dependence on diesel places a major strain on both the community of Saibai Island and the Torres Strait Regional Council (TSIRC). Keeping generators running has been proven expensive of residents as costs fallback on residents through higher costs of living. Deliveries are another problem for their current system, as during cyclone season or king tides, barges often can’t dock safely for supplies and more importantly fuel. When this happens, houses, schools and health care will lose power for days and even weeks. This is not only inconvenient but it can mean food spoiling, medical fridges warming and digital communication dropping all together as the Islands residents and system has become more reliant on energy in recent decades.

Saibai’s landscape makes this situation even harder to manage. Most of the island sits barely above sea level, so every high tide or heavy rain carries the risk of flooding. There’s simply not enough elevated ground to build new infrastructure, and the small amount of dry land that does exist is already needed for homes and community buildings

Attempts to bring solar into other Torres Strait communities, like on Masig Island, show both the promise and the limits of renewable projects. The technology works, but the systems still depend on outside contractors to maintain them. Because of that, local people don’t always get the chance to develop the technical skills or decision making control needed to run the systems themselves.
